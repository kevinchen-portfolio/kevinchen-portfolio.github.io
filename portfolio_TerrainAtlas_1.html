Antique Map    North America   1:11,000,000

The map covered the eastern half of North America from the Hudson Bay to the Gulf of Mexico. It showed boundaries, cities and towns, forts, native villages, and tribal territories in the 18th century.

With Adobe Photoshop, the map was coloured to distinguish political subdivisions, enhance appearance and increase readability.

Enhanced Hillshade     B.C. Canada   1 : 400,000

It showed Upper North Thompson River Lowlands in B.C. With ArcGIS Pro, digital elevation model (DEM) data was convered to a shaded relief raster by specifying the illumination source angle and shadows.

With Adobe Photoshop, hypsometric tints were combined to shaded relief to give the map’s depth and dimension, which displayed a better representation of the physical terrain.

Enhanced Hillshade With Filters   B.C. Canada  1 : 400,000

With Photoshop, this map brought styles and vibrance using various filters and effects. The Conté Crayon filter replicated dense dark and pure white Conté crayons on the image. It was embedded to make the map appear as if the textures were painted on.

The hydrographic data was added with the inner glow. This helped to simulate an embossed effect and create some depth within the water.

Sunrise Hillshade    Grand Canyon, United States   1:64,000

Followed by John Nelson’s “Hacking a DEM Sunrise” blog, hillshade, darkness, and mist layers were generated from DEM. The map created a vibrant gold sunrise with long dark shadows and misty fogs by overlapping the three layers.

With ArcGIS Pro, this 2D map was converted to a 3D oblique scene to mimic a magnificent sunrise in the world’s deepest and longest canyon.

Imhof Hillshade    Matterhorn, Switzerland and Italy   1:500,000

The map was made to honour a Swiss cartographer, Eduard Imhof, who created a style to portray topography in a particularly vivid manner.

With ArcGIS Pro, a hillshade was generalized and smoothed from a DEM. By grouping the hillshade raster with different transparencies hypsometric tints, a Swiss-style hillshade map was produced to depict the Matterhorn, one of the best-known mountains of the Alps.

Multi-Directional Hillshade  Glacier Peak, United States  1:80,000

Developed by Dr. Robert Mark, USGS, this Multidirectional hillshade method generated a surface from four different illumination angles. The workflow included smoothing DEM, eliminating null data, and summarizing all weighted raster data.

With ModelBuilder, all work processes can be encapsulated in a model and be ready to be used at all time. The 3D scene showed an oblique view of Glacier Peak, one of Washington’s most active volcanoes.
